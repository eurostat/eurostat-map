Eurostat Regional Data · 2023

The uneven geography of European prosperity

GDP per capita across EU regions ranges from under 50% to over 250% of the EU average — a gap that tells a story of divergence, catch-up, and persistent inequality.

Scroll to explore

01 — Overview

A continent of contrasts

The EU's single market conceals vast differences in regional wealth. GDP per capita, measured as a percentage of the EU average, reveals a sharp east–west divide — with a few exceptional outliers on both ends.

249 Luxembourg's GDP index — among the EU's highest values

02 — The rich north

Northern wealth clusters around capital cities

The highest-income regions are concentrated in a band from Ireland through the Benelux, Germany, and Austria into Scandinavia. Capital city regions dominate — Île-de-France, Brussels, Hamburg, and Stockholm all exceed 150 on the index.

Regions above 130 are highlighted.

03 — The lagging east

Eastern regions remain far behind

Despite decades of convergence funding, large swathes of Romania, Bulgaria, and parts of Poland and Hungary remain below 60 on the GDP index — qualifying for the highest tier of EU cohesion funding.

29 BG31 (north-west Bulgaria) — the lowest indexed region in the dataset

04 — By country

Within-country inequality is just as stark

The regions animate from the map into a dot chart grouped by country. Notice how France, Germany, and Ireland have enormous internal spreads, while smaller nations cluster tightly around their national average.

Each dot is a NUTS-2 region. The square marks the country's mean.

05 — The takeaway

Policy must address regional , not just national, gaps

National averages mask the real story. Poland's national GDP sits around 80% of the EU average — but Warsaw's region (PL91) exceeds 170, while its eastern periphery (PL84) sits at just 46.

120+ percentage point spread between Poland's richest and poorest regions
